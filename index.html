About Me

Designing Systems
That Think

I'm a game designer with a deep focus on systems design, player psychology, and rapid prototyping. My background spans graphic design, digital media, and game development — a combination that lets me approach problems from both a visual and mechanical perspective.

Currently studying Game Design at Image Campus, I work at the intersection of narrative systems, gameplay loops, and iterative prototyping. I believe great games are built in layers — each mechanic, interaction, and choice carefully designed to serve a larger emotional truth.

My process is grounded in player psychology: understanding not just what players do, but why they do it. I prototype fast, test relentlessly, and iterate with purpose — letting the design emerge through play rather than planning alone.

01 Systems first. Every mechanic should generate emergent behavior. I design rules, not outcomes.
02 Player psychology. The best mechanics tap into intrinsic motivation — curiosity, mastery, agency.
03 Prototype ruthlessly. Ideas are cheap. A working prototype reveals truth that documentation never will.

The Last
Way Out

3D First-Person Horror Escape · Unity

The Last Way Out is a compact first-person horror prototype set inside a dark industrial workshop. The core design challenge was communicating player objective and spatial readability without UI — every tool on the bench, warning light, and exit sign carries navigational meaning through placement, contrast, and sight lines rather than prompts or waypoints. Tension is built in layers: the physical workspace limits mobility and creates natural chokepoints; the warning light system drives rhythmic atmospheric pacing rather than constant dread; and the physics-based interaction system anchors the player to the space, making every object feel consequential. The escape loop is deliberately compact — designed to prove that a strong atmospheric system with readable objectives doesn't require scale to feel complete.

Lost Signal

Narrative Systems Prototype · Web

Lost Signal is a narrative systems prototype built around a deliberately unreliable narrator. The player is Dr. Mara Chen — crisis negotiator, Division 7 — but the Cognitive Reliability meter tracks something the briefing didn't mention: whether your version of events can be trusted. Dialogue choices don't just affect the subject on the roof; they surface inconsistencies in the player's own file. A field dossier panel lets you investigate restricted documents mid-conversation, introducing an evidence layer that quietly challenges everything you thought was established. Tension builds not from external threat, but from the player's growing uncertainty about their own position in the story.

STILL

Narrative Systemic Prototype · Unity

STILL is a systemic narrative prototype built around a single design question: what does an NPC actually remember? The world runs on a continuous simulation — NPCs carry persistent memory states, form opinions through repeated interactions, and maintain routines the player can observe before choosing to disrupt. Scripted moments were kept minimal so that emergent situations could reveal character. Player agency is real but leaves a mark — broken trust, witnessed actions, and unresolved tensions persist, making every decision feel consequential rather than reversible.

Roguelike
Shooter

Combat Loop Prototype · Unity

The Roguelike Shooter was a controlled experiment in combat feel — designed from the loop outward before any aesthetics were considered. The central design challenge was the gap between mechanical difficulty and perceived fairness: a run-ending moment should feel avoidable in retrospect, not arbitrary. Wave difficulty scales through enemy composition and pressure timing rather than stat inflation. Upgrade decisions are structured to compound — players commit to a build and live with it, which raises the stakes of every choice and makes each run feel distinct from the last.

void protocol

Stealth Systems Prototype · Unity

void protocol inverts the standard stealth power dynamic: the player enters a space already knowing enemy positions, patrol routes, and trigger conditions — but every action leaves a persistent trace. The core design tension is information asymmetry used against the player's own advantage. Alert escalation doesn't follow a binary detected/undetected state; it builds through a spectrum of suspicion and accumulated evidence, so careless play isn't punished instantly but compounds into inevitability. Players learn to spend their advantage carefully, treating each interaction as a resource.
